MULTI-TEMPORAL ANALYSIS OF RICE CROP HEALTH IN MWEA IRRIGATION SCHEME USING SENTINEL-2 IMAGERY (2025)

Background of the Study

  • 75% imported (30 billion KES per year), consumption at 1 million tonnes while local production is only 250,000 tonnes. Rice is the 3rd staple and fastest-growing demand at 12% annually.
  • Produces 60% of Kenya's rice across 26,000 acres in Kirinyaga County. Supports 7,500+ families across 5 sections (Karaba, Mwea, Tebere, Thiba, Wamumu) with 8 cropping systems.
  • Current visual inspection by extension officers cannot cover 7,500 plots. Problems detected only after visible symptoms appear when yield losses have already occurred.
  • Remote sensing detects crop stress before visible symptoms, covers entire scheme every 5 days, provides plot-level data, and enables evidence-based decisions for farmers and managers.

Study Area

Objectives

  1. To map the spatial distribution of rice crop health across 489+ plots in Mwea Irrigation Scheme using monthly NDVI data from 2025.
  2. To analyze temporal variations in vegetation health across two growing seasons and identify physiological patterns (planting, peak growth, harvest).
  3. To identify stress-prone areas within the scheme's five sections and provide evidence-based recommendations for targeted interventions.

Methods

A total of 489+ plots were assessed in the Mwea Irrigation Scheme, Kenya from January to December 2025.

Data & Processing

  • Sentinel-2 imagery (10m resolution, 5-day revisit)
  • 12 monthly composites (January-December 2025)
  • Cloud masking and median compositing in Google Earth Engine
  • NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red) — Greenness (0-1 scale)
  • NDWI = (Green - NIR) / (Green + NIR) — Vegetation water content
  • NDMI = (NIR - SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR) — Moisture stress detection
  • NDDI = (SWIR - Green) / (SWIR + Green) — Field flood mapping

Sampling

  • 7,500+ individual plot boundaries from National Irrigation Authority
  • Mean values for each extracted per plot per month
  • Total: 90,000+ data points per index (plots × 12 months)

Data Output

  • Wide-format CSV with monthly NDVI, NDMI, NDDI, NDWI indices
  • Ready for statistical analysis in Excel, R, or Python

Results and Discussion

Growing Seasons

  • Two distinct growing seasons observed: Long rains (March-May) and Short rains (October-December).
  • NDVI peaks in October (Short rains) and NDVI dips in June-September following the physiological pattern: planting → peak → harvest.

Rainfall vs NDVI

  • April rains (123mm) → May NDVI peak (0.59)
  • October rains (88mm) → November NDVI peak (0.52)
  • NDVI response follows rainfall by approximately 30-40 days
  • Dry periods show immediate decline in vegetation health

Key Findings

  • NDVI (crop health) and NDMI (moisture) show similar patterns
  • NDVI remains negative throughout the year
  • NDWI lowest during dry periods (no flooded fields)
  • All indices confirm two distinct cropping seasons

Long & Short Rains

  • Long rains (March-May): 280mm total, Peak: April (123mm)
  • Short rains (October-December): 160mm total, Peak: October (88mm)
  • Dry period (June-September): < 15mm/month
  • Minimal crop growth without irrigation

Spatial Distribution of Crop Health

The NDVI values reveal distinct patterns in crop health across the scheme:

  • Mwea block (7.59): Shows the highest rice vegetation, indicating optimal growing conditions, good water access, and effective management.
  • Karaba (6.69) & Tebere (6.23): Demonstrate moderately good crop health, benefiting from the scheme's potential.
  • CRITICAL FINDING: Karaba (5.40) and Wamumu (5.92) recorded the lowest NDVI values, indicating poor crop health and stress conditions.

Overall Assessment

Critical areas requiring immediate intervention:

  • Karaba (5.40) — Lowest NDVI, highest stress
  • Wamumu (5.92) — Second lowest, significant stress

Conclusion

This study assessed rice crop health across six blocks in Mwea Irrigation Scheme using NDVI from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery. Results revealed significant variability in vegetation health, with Mwea block recording the highest NDVI (7.59), indicating optimal growing conditions. Karaba (6.69) and Tebere (6.23) showed moderately good crop health, while Wamumu (5.92) and Karaba (5.40) recorded the lowest values, identifying them as stress-prone areas.

Karaba and Wamumu blocks require priority interventions including water access assessment, soil fertility testing, and targeted support. This study demonstrates that remote sensing enables timely, plot-level crop health assessment, providing evidence for targeted resource allocation and improved agricultural management.
